Materials and construction

The mat base is needle-felted from New Zealand wool, chosen for its short staple and high lanolin content. The coral and anemones are needle-felted onto the base with a third layer of wool to give the structures rigidity. The yellow starfish is felted separately and removable. The pink clam is hand-stitched and houses a small felted pearl, also removable for supervised older children.

All yarn is OEKO-TEX certified. Designed in Australia by Drewart, ethically produced in their privately owned factory in China.

Ways to play

Small-world play with wooden sea creatures and felt-friendly figures. Tide-pool storytelling. Texture exploration for younger children. Combine with the Busy Bee Tray for a tray-based scene with a built-in border.

Skills it builds

Tactile discrimination across three distinct felt textures and three structural heights. Fine motor control through pincer-grip placement of the removable starfish and pearl. Narrative imagination that scales from age two to age nine. Color and marine vocabulary: anemone, clam, coral, starfish.

Who it is for

Open-ended-play homes. Reggio and Montessori households use this mat as the invitation surface for sensory and small-world work. Homeschool families building marine-biology units. Grandparents looking for a gift that lasts. Therapists and educators. Adults who care about how a room looks.

Care and storage

Spot clean only. Use a damp cloth with cool water and a small amount of wool-safe soap. Do not submerge for prolonged periods. Air dry flat away from direct sunlight. Store flat or rolled in a clean cotton bag away from moths.

Meet the maker

Drewart · Saxony, Germany

Drewart is a small workshop in Saxony, Germany, founded in 1985. They make wooden vehicles and dollhouses by hand, one piece at a time, in solid maple, alder, and beech. The pieces are hand-finished with food-safe beeswax and natural plant-based oils, and every wheel is turned on a wooden spindle. No paint, no varnish, no plastic. The grain deepens with handling, which is part of why a Drewart toy looks better at age ten than it did at age one.
