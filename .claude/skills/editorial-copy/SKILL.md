# Editorial Copy — Premium Writing Intelligence

A discipline for writing copy that reads like a good magazine, not a brochure. Use it to draft new copy, rewrite weak copy, or critique existing copy against a consistent quality bar.

> This is the **general base layer**. Brand-specific rules (voice, naming, claims, banned phrases) belong in a companion brand skill that extends this one — e.g. `mtw-brand-voice`. When both apply, the brand skill **overrides** this one on any conflict.

## When to Apply

### Must Use
- Writing or rewriting any customer-facing prose: hero/cover ledes, intros, product blurbs, bundle copy, pull quotes, headlines, meta descriptions, email subjects, CTAs.
- Someone says copy feels "flat", "generic", "salesy", "AI-written", or "not premium enough."
- Polishing for launch or reviewing a batch of copy for consistency.

### Recommended
- Naming sections, products, or collections.
- Deciding tone for a new page type.
- Auditing a page for claim accuracy and filler.

### Skip
- Pure code, config, or data work.
- Legal/compliance text that must be quoted verbatim.

## The 9 principles of premium copy

1. **Specific beats vague.** Replace adjectives with facts and nouns. Not "beautifully crafted" → "turned on a foot-powered lathe." Detail is the cheapest luxury signal there is.
2. **Show the thing, don't praise the thing.** Don't say "kids love it." Show the scene: "Sets it on the windowsill so the light comes through." Let the reader conclude.
3. **One idea per sentence.** Premium prose is confident enough to use short sentences. Cut "and … and …" chains into separate beats.
4. **Earn the emotion.** Sentiment lands only after a concrete detail sets it up. Detail first, feeling second — never feeling alone.
5. **Active voice, present tense, real verbs.** "Wool holds warmth" beats "warmth is retained by the wool." Hunt down *is/are/was* + noun and replace with a verb.
6. **Cut the hedge words.** Delete *very, really, truly, simply, just, quite, beautiful, amazing, perfect, premium, high-quality, unique, carefully.* If the sentence survives the cut, it was filler.
7. **Rhythm is a feature.** Vary sentence length. A long, textured sentence followed by three words. Read it aloud — if you run out of breath or it drones, recut.
8. **Concrete sensory nouns.** Name materials, places, sounds, weights, temperatures. "Beechwood," "Kathmandu," "the click of the latch." Senses make copy feel observed, not invented.
9. **Every claim must be true and sourced.** No origin, certification, material, or superlative claim goes in unless verified. A single wrong fact reads as the whole brand lying.

## Structures (with the job each does)

**Hero / cover lede** — 1–3 sentences. Job: place the reader in a world and signal the category, fast. Lead with image-language, not a sales pitch. End on a line that earns a small breath.

**Editorial intro (long-form, often dropcap)** — 2–4 short paragraphs. Job: give the page a point of view. Open on a concrete scene or a maker detail, widen to why it matters, land on the reader's situation. Never open with "Welcome to" or "At [Brand], we believe."

**Product blurb** — 1–2 sentences under a name. Job: one true, specific, useful thing the photo can't say (material, origin, play pattern, scale, who it's for). Not a feature list.

**Bundle copy** — a short headline + a line. Job: explain the *idea* of the grouping ("Everything a tray of sand wants"), not the SKU contents.

**Pull quote / review** — lift the most specific, least generic line. "My 3-year-old named every gnome" beats "Great product, fast shipping."

**Eyebrow / chip / label** — tiny. Job: orient (section number, category, count). No selling. Keep brand-naming rules strict here (see brand skill).

**Meta description** — ≤155 chars. Job: mirror the cover lede's promise in plain, searchable language. Must stay in sync with the on-page copy.

## Working process

1. **Read the surrounding copy first.** Match the existing voice, density, and rhythm — new copy should be indistinguishable from the best paragraph already on the page.
2. **Find the one true detail.** Before writing, identify the most specific real fact (material, place, maker, play pattern). Build the line around it.
3. **Draft hot, then cut cold.** Write freely, then delete every word from principle #6. Most first drafts lose 30%.
4. **Read it aloud.** Fix anything you stumble on. Rhythm problems only show up out loud.
5. **Fact-check every claim.** Origin, certification, material, count, superlative — verify each before it ships.
6. **Diff against the bar.** Run the checklist below.

## Quality checklist
- [ ] No banned filler words (very/really/simply/beautiful/premium/high-quality/unique…).
- [ ] At least one concrete sensory noun per paragraph.
- [ ] No "is/are + noun" where a verb would do.
- [ ] Sentence lengths vary; reads cleanly aloud.
- [ ] Every factual claim is verified.
- [ ] Emotion (if any) is preceded by a concrete detail.
- [ ] Meta description matches on-page copy.
- [ ] Matches the voice of the surrounding page.

## Before / after (the standard)
- ❌ "Our beautifully crafted, high-quality wooden toys are loved by kids everywhere."
- ✅ "Turned from a single block of beech and finished with beeswax. It picks up the warmth of a hand."
